# Air Fryer Probe Meat Temperature Chart
## Redesign Proposal

**Project:** Meat Temperature Chart Redesign  
**Client:** Cribbys Ltd (Ben Cribb)  
**Date:** April 24, 2026  
**Status:** Design Strategy & Recommendations

---

## Executive Summary

The current chart tries to do too much (quick reference + instruction manual + safety guide) and succeeds at none of it. The redesign simplifies ruthlessly: one safe temperature per meat, bold typography, zero instruction clutter on the front. Single version ships with all products (Shopify + Amazon), but the design must satisfy both lifestyle shoppers and practical buyers simultaneously.

The solution is clean, professional, scannable from distance, and removes all cognitive friction between "what meat am I cooking" and "what temperature should it be."

---

## Design Philosophy

**Primary Goal:** Create a chart that answers one question instantly: "What temperature should this meat be?"

**Secondary Goal:** Look professional and trustworthy to both Shopify shoppers (seeking confidence/lifestyle) and Amazon shoppers (seeking practical reliability).

**Core Principle:** Temperatures are the hero. Everything else exists to support them. Remove anything that competes for attention.

---

## What's Wrong with the Current Design

1. **Temperatures aren't prominent.** They're the same visual weight as the meat names and doneness levels. Users have to scan and process multiple levels of information to find one number.

2. **Doneness levels create confusion.** Rare/Medium/Well Done—which one am I supposed to use? This adds cognitive load. For safety-critical information (meat thermometers), there should be one answer, not options.

3. **Right side is information overload.** "Guide to Reading Internal Temperatures" + "Important" section + hygiene warnings = instruction manual aesthetic. This kills the clean, professional feeling Shopify customers want and buries the core reference data Amazon customers need.

4. **Text is genuinely too small.** Both the meat type descriptors and the instruction blocks are hard to read at normal distances. Client feedback is accurate: "text needs to be bigger."

5. **Visual hierarchy is broken.** There's no clear path for the eye. Is it meat → temperature? Meat → doneness → temperature? The journey should be instant.

6. **It doesn't work from distance.** You can't read this from across the kitchen. For a product that's supposed to live on/near an air fryer, that's a critical failure.

7. **White border frames seem accidental.** They add visual noise without serving a purpose. Client wants to remove them.

## Design Strategy: Two Approaches

### Option 1: Landscape (Horizontal) — RECOMMENDED

**Format:** Approximately 21cm wide × 13cm tall (magnetic PVC, wipe-clean)

**Visual Layout:**
- Imagine the card divided into three zones: left, center, right
- **Top band (full width):** Minimal header with "MEAT TEMPERATURE GUIDE" in clean, bold sans-serif. Small gray subheader underneath: "Safe internal temperatures for perfectly cooked food." Right side of this band has the Air Fryer Probe logo/brand in orange.
- **Main content area:** Six rows, each representing a meat type
  - Left of each row: meat icon (visual scanability aid)
  - Center of each row: meat type name in medium-weight, readable sans-serif
  - Right of each row: THE TEMPERATURE in absolutely massive, bold, high-contrast orange numerals

**Typography Details:**
- Header: 18-20pt, bold, white, clean sans-serif (Helvetica, Inter, or similar)
- Subheader: 10-11pt, light gray, sans-serif (supporting information, not primary focus)
- Meat names: 14-16pt, white, semi-bold sans-serif (easy to scan, not aggressive)
- **Temperatures: 24-28pt, bold, orange (#ff9500), sans-serif** ← This is the visual anchor. These numbers should dominate the card.
- Logo: 14-16pt, orange, positioned bottom-right or top-right

**Color Palette:**
- Background: True black (#000000)
- Primary text: White (#ffffff)
- Temperatures: Warm orange (#ff9500) — contrasts sharply with black, draws the eye, aligns with brand
- Dividers: Very dark gray (#444444) — subtle horizontal lines between meat rows, not jarring

**Why Landscape Works:**
- Maintains the horizontal proportion of the current card, so it still fits standard magnetic attachment points on air fryers
- The left-to-right reading pattern (icon → meat → temperature) feels natural
- Horizontal proportions give you enough space to make text actually readable without cramping
- Professional, clean, modern—doesn't look like an instruction manual
- Works well for both "quick reference from across the kitchen" (Amazon shopper) and "confident, clean tool" (Shopify shopper)

**Key Design Decisions:**
- No doneness levels (Rare/Medium/Well Done). Choose one safe minimum temperature per meat and stick with it.
- No instruction text on front ("Insert probe into thickest part..."). Zero. This isn't an instruction manual.
- No safety warnings on front ("Wash thoroughly after each use..."). Move this to the back if needed, but don't clutter the front.
- Meat icons add visual interest and scanning aid without adding text.
- Orange temperatures create visual hierarchy and brand connection without being garish.
- Horizontal dividers between rows, not boxes around each row (cleaner, less cluttered).

---

### Option 2: Portrait (Vertical) — ALTERNATIVE

**Format:** Approximately 13cm wide × 21cm tall (magnetic PVC, wipe-clean)

**Visual Layout:**
- **Top section (full width):** Header "MEAT TEMPERATURE GUIDE" centered, with small subheader below. Very similar to landscape but vertically stacked.
- **Main content area:** Six rows stacked vertically, each representing a meat type. More generous vertical spacing than landscape.
  - Left: Meat icon (slightly larger since there's more room)
  - Center: Meat type name
  - Right: Temperature in massive, bold orange
- **Bottom section:** Logo/brand, centered or bottom-right

**Why Portrait Could Work:**
- Extra vertical space means even more generous text sizing—temperatures could go 28-32pt without crowding
- Better for fridge mounting (traditional kitchen reference chart placement) or overhead mounting on/above air fryer
- More breathing room overall—less cramped feeling
- Still maintains the clean, professional aesthetic
- The vertical arrangement could feel more like a "list" which some people intuitively scan better

**Why I'd Choose Landscape Instead:**
- Landscape is closer to the current format, so it requires less retooling of packaging/product photos
- Horizontal proportions are more standard for edge-mounted magnetic charts on appliances
- You get enough space for readable text without having to go portrait
- The horizontal flow (left to right, meat to temperature) is more intuitive

## Information Architecture: What To Keep, What To Cut

### KEEP on Front:
- Meat temperature chart (core data—this is the whole point)
- Meat type names + icons
- Safe internal temperatures (one per meat, no variants)
- "Meat Temperature Guide" title/header
- Logo/brand (small, doesn't compete)

### REMOVE from Front (or Move to Back):
- Doneness levels (Rare/Medium/Well Done) — confusing, creates cognitive load
- Instruction text ("Insert probe into centre of thickest part...") — this isn't an instruction manual
- Food safety warnings ("Wash probe thoroughly after each use...") — move to back
- "How to read internal temperatures" explanatory text — move to back
- "Important" section about cross-contamination — move to back or packaging

### OPTIONAL: Two-Sided Design
If you want to preserve the educational/safety content without cluttering the front:
- **Front side:** Clean reference chart (meat type + temperature, nothing else)
- **Back side:** Instructions, safety info, cooking tips, brand story
This gives you the best of both: clean reference + comprehensive information

Current chart includes:
- Chicken & Poultry: 74°C (UK Food Safety Standard)
- Fish & Seafood: 63°C
- Pork: 70°C
- Beef & Lamb: 52°C (medium rare) — but recommend confirming if client prefers rare/medium/well versions, or a single safe minimum

**Recommendation:** Verify these against current UK Food Safety Standards and confirm with client. For beef specifically, decide: is 52°C (medium rare) the safe minimum, or should it be higher? This matters for liability/safety messaging.

## Typography Recommendations

**Font Family:** Clean, modern sans-serif
- Primary recommendation: Inter (open source, excellent readability, modern)
- Alternatives: Helvetica Neue, SF Pro Display, Roboto (Google), or any contemporary sans-serif
- Avoid: Anything serif, anything decorative, anything thin/light that reduces contrast

**Hierarchy (by visual weight and size):**

1. **Temperatures** (LARGEST) — 24-28pt, bold weight, orange color
   - These should be the first thing your eye lands on
   - Bold weight ensures they're not competing with other text
   - Orange color pops against black background
   - Generous letter-spacing optional (makes numbers feel more premium)

2. **Meat type names** (MEDIUM) — 14-16pt, semi-bold or regular weight, white
   - Clear and readable, but subordinate to temperatures
   - Sans-serif to keep modern feel
   - Regular or semi-bold (not bold, as that competes with temps)

3. **Header** (MEDIUM-LARGE) — 18-20pt, bold, white
   - "MEAT TEMPERATURE GUIDE" or similar
   - Should feel authoritative but not aggressive
   - Bold weight signals this is important information

4. **Subheader** (SMALL) — 10-11pt, regular weight, light gray (#aaa or #bbb)
   - "Safe internal temperatures for perfectly cooked food" or similar
   - Optional; can omit if space is tight
   - Should be clearly subordinate (smaller, lower contrast)

5. **Logo/Brand** (SMALL-MEDIUM) — 14-18pt, bold, orange
   - "AIR FRYER PROBE" or similar brand attribution
   - Positioned to not interfere with main content (corner, usually)
   - Orange ties it to the temperature numbers, creates visual cohesion

**Line Length:** N/A (no paragraphs on this chart, just labels/numbers)

**Line Height/Spacing:** Generous. Between meat rows, create visible white/black space. Don't cram them together.

## Information Hierarchy: The User's Eye Journey

**Current chart** (broken): Eye lands randomly. Is it meat? Is it the instructions? Confusion.

**Redesigned chart** (goal): Clear, instant path.

1. User looks at chart
2. User scans for the meat they're cooking (icons + meat names help)
3. User reads the temperature next to it (large orange number, impossible to miss)
4. User looks at their probe's reading and cooks to that temperature
5. Done.

This should take 2 seconds max.

## Design Decisions & Rationale

**Why no doneness levels?**
Doneness levels (Rare/Medium/Well Done) add confusion to a safety-critical decision. One safe minimum temperature is clearer, safer, and less cognitively demanding. If the client wants to preserve doneness guidance, move it to the back side or packaging, not the main chart.

**Why no instruction text on front?**
Instruction text ("Insert probe into centre of thickest part...") makes the chart feel like a manual, not a reference tool. Users already know how to use a thermometer. The probe's packaging and the product itself can carry instructions. The chart's job is to answer "what temperature?"

**Why orange for temperatures?**
Orange creates visual separation without being harsh. It's warm (appeals to Shopify's lifestyle positioning) and bold (appeals to Amazon's practical positioning). It ties to the brand color, creating visual cohesion.

**Why landscape over portrait?**
Landscape maintains the current proportions, making it easier to update existing packaging/photography. You get enough space for readable text without going full portrait. Horizontal flow feels more natural for this type of reference (left = what you're cooking, right = temperature).

## Implementation Notes

**Physical Production:**
- Material: Wipe-clean PVC (durable, magnetic backing)
- Finish: Matte (prevents glare when reading)
- Magnetic backing: Strong enough to stick to metal air fryer surfaces
- Dimensions: Confirm exact final size with client and packaging constraints
- Print resolution: 300dpi minimum for crisp text

**Icon Selection:**
- Recommend simple, bold, recognizable icons for each meat type
- Options: Food emoji (simple but less premium), illustrated icons (professional but requires designer), minimalist silhouettes (modern, clean)
- Icons should be 20-24px at print size, bold enough to be recognizable from distance

**Testing:**
- Print a prototype at full size
- Test readability from 1-2 meters away
- Confirm text sizes feel right in person (screen vs. print often feel different)
- Verify color contrast meets accessibility standards
- Check that magnetic backing is strong enough
